# Study A — Proposer Brief (designers)

**You are creating wall-design proposals that other experts will evaluate blind. Follow this sheet exactly. Do not read the reviewer form. Do not ask which "class" your proposal should be.**

## 1. Purpose (what this is for)
We are testing whether a design framework can tell apart great, competent, and generic wall designs. Your job is to produce real proposals across a range of ambition. You are **not** guessing what a homeowner would buy — only doing your best design work.

## 2. What you receive
- The 6 room photos: **R1–R6** (R1 blank-oversized wall · R2 off-centre-window imbalance · R3 busy-needs-calm · R4 ignored fireplace · R5 dark/small · R6 generic-no-identity).
- The fixed **catalogue** (~40–60 buyable pieces, with sizes + frame options).
- The **Room Diagnosis Schema** (Artifact 1) and **Thesis Library** (Artifact 2, T1–T26).
- Your proposer ID: **D_ (assigned)**.

## 3. Your task, per assigned room
For **each** room you're assigned, create **two required proposals** (optionally a third):
- **(a) SAFE proposal** — the expected, low-risk, "won't offend anyone" choice. What a competent shop would suggest. Correct, unsurprising.
- **(b) BOLD-BUT-RESOLVED proposal** — your best, most confident design: a clear point of view that still *resolves the whole room*.
- **(c) optional — BOLD-BUT-UNSURE** — an ambitious idea you're genuinely not sure resolves.

### SAFE — definition
Passes physical validity and looks competent; has **no strong single idea**; would be most people's "sensible" pick; minimises risk and surprise.

### BOLD-BUT-RESOLVED — definition
Passes physical validity **and** competence, **and** commits to one clear idea that makes the *whole room* feel more intentional. Non-obvious, but once seen it feels right. Boldness only counts **after** correctness is secured — a bold idea that breaks scale/placement/competence does not qualify.

## 4. Method you must follow (do it in this order)
1. **Diagnose** the room using the Schema — write the room's actual problem.
2. **Thesis** — one sentence of design intent (you may draw on T1–T26).
3. **Intervention** — choose the archetype (single statement / gallery / asymmetry / mirror / shelf / restraint-empty / etc.) that solves the diagnosed problem.
4. **Composition** — resolve scale, placement, balance, focal hierarchy, negative space.
5. **Products last** — pick specific catalogue pieces to realise the composition.

## 5. Catalogue & sellability rules
- Use **only** the provided catalogue for the specific pieces.
- If your ideal intervention needs something **not sellable** (mirror, shelf, moulding, lighting, leaving-empty): you **may** propose it, but you **must tag it `DESIGNABLE-NOT-SELLABLE`** on the metadata sheet and note what's missing. Do not silently swap to a lesser sellable idea — flag the gap.

## 6. Render requirements
- One image: the **room photo with your design composited on it** to scale and perspective.
- Use the shared render template / operator so all cards look equally finished (do not add polish others can't match — we're judging design, not Photoshop).
- Neutral background/lighting as in the original photo; no added props.

## 8. What you must NOT do
- ✗ Do **not** read the reviewer form or scoring criteria.
- ✗ Do **not** collaborate with or view other proposers' work.
- ✗ Do **not** label your render with your name, thesis, or type — that lives on the hidden sheet only.
- ✗ Do **not** produce two near-identical proposals (cosmetic variants). SAFE and BOLD must be **materially different** in idea/archetype.
- ✗ Do **not** try to guess or engineer a "class."

## 9. Submission checklist (per proposal)

- [ ] Followed diagnose → thesis → intervention → composition → products

- [ ] SAFE and BOLD are materially different
